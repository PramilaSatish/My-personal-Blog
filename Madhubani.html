INDIAN TRADITIONAL ART-MADHUBANI ART.

Madhubani painting is a folk art of the Mithila region of Bihar, India. This style of painting is characterized by its use of bright colors, geometric patterns, and stylized figures. Madhubani paintings are often used to decorate walls, floors, and furniture. They are also used to illustrate religious texts and stories.

Named after the Madhubani district of Bihar, this print is known as ‘Mithila’ and is done by hand on materials ranging from cotton to silk. It features bright coloured drawings of lines that characteristically contrast and form a unique pattern. Bright colours are traditionally formulated with natural pigments and dyes like ochre and red. The print can be made with various tools, including twigs, matchsticks, brushes, and pen nibs.

Tracing Its Origin

The origin of the Madhubani print can be traced back to the Ramayana, over 2500 years ago. The King of Mithila, Janaka, asked an artist to paint his daughter Sita’s wedding to Prince Rama. Women typically did paintings on walls and floors of homes for such special occasions with a paste made from powdered rice. William G. Archer, the British Colonial Officer in charge of the Madhubani district, officially discovered this print in 1934 when a massive earthquake hit Bihar. He came across the wall paintings while inspecting the damage caused by the natural disaster.
Traditionally done on walls (bhittichitra) and floors (aripana), Madhubani art is now also done on clothes, papers, and canvases (patachitra). The art skills were passed down from generation to generation by women who painted people with natural and mythological elements from ancient epics like the Ramayana and Mahabharata.

Features of Madhubani Painting

Madhubani paintings can be either large, like frescoes, or small, like miniatures.Madhubani painting designs is characterized by line drawings filled in with bright contrasting colors and patterns. The paintings are divided into horizontal and vertical sections to show different times and places.These are renowned for their simplicity since the colors and brushes used are frequently made from natural materials. There are also paintings representing royal court settings and social gatherings such as weddings.The use of geometrical patterns is quite obvious in these paintings. The fact that these sophisticated mathematical patterns were utilized in Madhubani paintings adds to their allure and uniqueness. The traditional Madhubani Paintings are renowned for using geometric patterns. The Madhubani painting designs depicts ceremonial themes for certain events, such as festivals, religious rites, etc. The bodies of gods and goddesses are often distorted and foreshortened. This is common in Radha-Krishna and Ganesha Madhubani paintings. The faces are shown in profile, but the eyes are painted in a frontal view. The eyes are usually painted last.

Significant Symbols in Madhubani Paintings

Some significant symbols commonly seen in Madhubani paintings are:

Fish:

The fish symbolizes fertility, prosperity, and good fortune. It represents the presence of Goddess Laxmi. Fish are often depicted in pairs in Madhubani art.

Lotus flower:

The lotus flower symbolizes purity, enlightenment, and spiritual unfolding. It is associated with the deity Lord Vishnu.

Peacock:

The peacock represents beauty, grace, and royalty. It symbolizes immortality because its feathers don't decay even after the peacock dies.

Sun and moon:

The sun and moon represent opposing yet complementary energies of masculine and feminine, light and darkness.

Elephants:

Elephants symbolize strength, wisdom, and good luck. They also represent royalty and are seen as protectors.

Snake:

Snakes, especially coiled ones, represent eternity and the cycle of life. They also symbolize and protect against evil forces.

Tiger:

The tiger symbolizes courage, power, valor, and ferocity. It is seen as a protector and guardian of Madhubani art.

Mango tree:

The mango tree represents fertility, fortune, and abundance. Its fruits are a symbol of joy, health and prosperity.

Crow:

The crow often seen perched on a mango tree symbolizes wisdom, intelligence, and alertness.

Making Process of Madhubani Painting

  • Colors in Madhubani paintings are sourced from plants and other natural sources and are combined and processed to produce the desired colors. The artists themselves create these colors.
  • Soot mixed with cow dung produces black, turmeric produces yellow, indigo produces blue, flower juice produces red, red sandalwood produces red, leaves produce green, rice flour produces white, and palasha flowers produce orange.
  • Cotton is wrapped around a bamboo stick instead of modern brushes to manufacture brushes.
  • The traditional Madhubani Painting designs is also made with objects such as twigs, matchsticks, and fingers. Madhubani paintings are created on mud walls, floors, paper, cloth, canvas, and other materials.
  • Empty spaces are frequently filled with floral, animal, bird, and geometrical pattern motifs, and the painting is finished with double lines painted along the border.

    • Materials Used in Madhubani Paintings

    Paper: Mithila paintings used to adorn walls with mud and cow dung as base, but from the late 1960s, handmade paper became a canvas of choice. Handmade paper is crafted using cow dung, neem juice, and multani mitti, giving it a mud wall-like appearance and protection from termites and insects. Today, readily available light yellow handmade paper is directly used for Madhubani paintings.

    Colors: Madhubani paintings feature natural, vibrant colors like red, yellow, blue, and more. Some natural sources for these colors include:

    • Black: Soot mixed with cow dung for borders and outlines.
    • Yellow: Turmeric for yellow hues.
    • White: Rice powder for white.
    • Green: Wood apple tree leaves for green.
    • Blue: Indigo for blue shades.
    • Red: Kusum flowers or red sandalwood for red.
    • Orange: Tesu flowers from the Palash tree for orange.

      • While natural colors are traditional, fabric colors are also used for durability, although some artists still adhere to traditional methods.

      Tools: Outlines are created with nib pens and brushes, with natural or fabric colors filled in using brushes, twigs, matchsticks, and even fingers.
